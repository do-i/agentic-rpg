# Chronicles of the Lost Flame — High-Level Scenario

A high-level narrative outline for the **Rusted Kingdoms** scenario. This document describes story shape only — themes, beats, and per-arc payoffs — not dialogue, quest steps, map geometry, or mechanics. It is intended as a compass for content authors (dialogue, encounters, bosses, items) so that individual scenes pull in the same direction.

---

## Main Theme

> *"The age of flame has rusted. What remains is ours to rekindle — or to bury with honor."*

Long ago, the **Eternal Flame** was the covenant between humankind and the world: a divine fire that warmed the hearths of five kingdoms, powered their sacred forges, and held back the Deep Cold that sleeps beneath the continent. Generations ago, the Flame began to *rust* — dimming, flaking, leaving iron-colored scars on the land, the sky, and the hearts of people.

The kingdoms rotted with it. Kings turned to tyrants, knights to mercenaries, prayers to superstition. Monsters born of *oxidized* magic — *Rustkin* — now creep out of the margins. The church insists the Flame can be relit; the academies insist it was never divine to begin with; the common folk just want to get through winter.

**Aric**, a village youth marked at birth by an ember that refuses to die, is drawn into a journey that forces the world to answer a harder question than *"can the Flame be relit?"* — namely, *"should it be?"* The game's central tension is between **restoration** (rekindle the old covenant) and **release** (let the age end so a new one can begin). The ending earned by the player reflects which truth they lived out.

**Structural note.** The outline below maps onto the four engine-managed acts (`story_act2_started` → `story_act4_started`, `boss_zone10_defeated`). Sub-epics 1–2 form Act I, 3–4 Act II, 5–6 Act III, 7–8 Act IV/finale.

## Sub-Epic 1 — The Ember That Would Not Die

**Setting.** Ardel, a quiet farming town at the edge of the Starting Forest.

Aric lives an ordinary life in Ardel, apprenticed to the village smith, teased for the small ember that hovers at his fingertips when he sleeps — a curiosity, nothing more. Then the forest *rusts overnight*: trees grey, beasts twist, and a Rustkin horde descends on the town. In defending a childhood friend, Aric's ember flares into a true flame for the first time, repelling the creatures but burning the village shrine to cinders.

The village elder reveals what she has kept silent for sixteen years: Aric was found as an infant at the shrine, swaddled in ash. The nearest answers lie north, in **Millhaven**, where a traveling scholar named **Elise** is asking uncomfortable questions about the old Flame.

**Arc conclusion.** Aric leaves Ardel not as a chosen hero but as a boy with a question he cannot un-ask. Elise joins him on the road — skeptical, curious, and already one step ahead of him.

## Sub-Epic 2 — The Scholar and the Soldier

**Setting.** Millhaven (a mill-and-market town) and the Open Plains between it and the coast.

Millhaven is prosperous and proud, but its prosperity is built on a secret: its grain mills are powered by a captured fragment of the old Flame, stolen generations ago from a ruined monastery. The fragment is *failing*. Mill barons have hired **Jep**, a discharged soldier turned sellsword, to "secure the situation" — meaning silence anyone who notices.

Aric and Elise stumble into the conspiracy. Jep, sent to kill them, instead turns on his employers when he recognizes in Aric's flame the same light he once saw at a battlefield massacre he survived and has never spoken of. The three expose the mill barons; the fragment is lost in the confrontation, and Millhaven's economy begins a slow, visible decline.

**Arc conclusion.** The party of three is formed. Aric learns his flame is not unique — *fragments* of the old Flame are scattered across the kingdoms, hoarded, weaponized, or worshipped. The scholar's road becomes a pilgrimage. **(Triggers Act II.)**

---

## Sub-Epic 3 — The Port of Half-Truths

**Setting.** Port town **Harborgate**, gateway to the wider continent.

Harborgate is where the three kingdoms' rumors collide: a free port, neutral, corrupt, and alive. The party comes to book passage north and finds the harbor locked down — a plague of "iron lung," said to be carried by refugees from the rust-sickened interior, has the city quarantined. The truth is uglier: the plague is a fabrication by the harbormaster's guild to justify seizing refugee ships.

The party meets **Reiya**, a priestess-in-exile tending the quarantined sick in defiance of guild orders. She is the first to name what Aric carries — not a curse, not a blessing, but a **Vessel-flame**, one of seven said to have been scattered when the covenant broke. She also carries grief of her own: her order was destroyed for teaching this very doctrine.

**Arc conclusion.** The quarantine is broken, the harbormaster's lie exposed, and Reiya joins the party — not to serve Aric, but to bear witness. The party sails north. For the first time, Aric hears the word **"Vessel"** applied to himself, and it does not feel like a gift.

---

## Sub-Epic 4 — The Watcher on the Ruin

**Setting.** **Ruinwatch**, a town built into the bones of a dead monastery on a cliff above the sea.

Ruinwatch is where the old Flame was said to have first gone to rust. The town survives by selling relics to pilgrims; its people are tired of hope. Here the party meets **Kael**, a young knight of a disbanded order, the last sworn sword of a dead saint. Kael is hunting a figure called **the Cinder Marshal** — a former paladin who now believes the Flame should be *extinguished*, not relit, and who has been collecting Vessel-flames across the continent and snuffing them.

The Cinder Marshal ambushes the party in the monastery's vaults. Aric's flame is nearly taken. Kael intervenes; the Marshal escapes, wounded, leaving behind a confession the party does not yet understand: *"I have met your kind before, boy. I buried the last one."*

**Arc conclusion.** Kael joins the party, her oath redirected — no longer to a dead saint, but to seeing this story through. The party now knows they are *not the first* attempt at this journey. Someone walked this road before Aric. Someone failed. **(Triggers Act III.)**

---

## Sub-Epic 5 — The Kingdom Beneath the Frost

**Setting.** **Frostholm**, a mountain kingdom that has survived the rusting by *freezing itself in time* — literally, through a pact with the Deep Cold.

Frostholm is beautiful and wrong. Its people do not age. Its king has reigned for three centuries. It is the only kingdom that has not declined — because it has not *moved*. The party comes seeking the third Vessel-flame, said to be held in the royal vault, and finds a court that will not let them leave.

Here the story's moral axis tilts. Frostholm is *proof* that the world can be preserved — at the cost of everything that makes a world worth preserving. The king offers Aric a place at his side: give up the journey, give up the flame, and live forever in a kingdom that will never rust. Elise is tempted. Jep is not. Reiya names it blasphemy. Kael draws her sword.

The party escapes with the Vessel-flame; Frostholm's pact cracks behind them, and the kingdom begins, at last, to age — a mercy or a murder, depending on who tells the story afterward.

**Arc conclusion.** The party has their first real moral wound: they left a kingdom worse than they found it, for reasons they believe in. Aric now understands that *restoration* and *release* are not abstractions — they are choices with bodies attached.

---

## Sub-Epic 6 — The Veil of Ash

**Setting.** **Ashenveil**, a city of mourners built on the site of the old capital, ruled by an oracle.

Ashenveil is where the covenant was broken. The oracle who rules it is, it turns out, the **first Vessel** — the one the Cinder Marshal spoke of — ancient, half-rusted herself, preserved by the very flame she failed to rekindle. She tells Aric the truth the journey has been circling:

- The Flame was never divine. It was a *compact* — a chosen human burned their life to keep the world warm.
- Each Vessel is that chosen human, reborn.
- The rust is not a sickness. It is the compact *refusing to renew itself*. The world is asking to be let go.
- The Cinder Marshal is not a villain. He is her former companion, who made the opposite choice — to end the compact mercifully, rather than force another child to burn.

Aric is offered the ritual. Elise, Jep, Reiya, and Kael are each given, privately, the option to stop him.

**Arc conclusion.** The party leaves Ashenveil fractured. No one has betrayed anyone, but no one agrees. The road to the final zone is walked in near silence. **(Triggers Act IV.)**

---

## Sub-Epic 7 — The Marshal's Last Stand

**Setting.** The rusted wastes approaching the final zone; a battlefield graveyard where the Cinder Marshal has made his camp.

The Cinder Marshal does not ambush the party this time. He is waiting. He has been waiting, he says, for a long time. He does not want to fight Aric — he wants to *convince* him. The confrontation is as much dialogue as combat: each companion speaks, each is answered, and the Marshal's grief is laid bare. He is not evil. He is simply older than hope.

The battle, when it comes, is a duel of conviction. If the party has leaned toward *release* across their journey, the Marshal yields and joins them as an ally for the final descent. If they have leaned toward *restoration*, he fights to the end, and his dying words are a blessing the party did not expect: *"Then carry it better than I did."*

**Arc conclusion.** The last obstacle between the party and the final zone is removed — not by defeating an enemy, but by understanding one. Whatever Aric chooses in the final hour, he will choose it knowing what it costs.

---

## Sub-Epic 8 — The Hearth at the End of the World

**Setting.** **Zone 10** — the Hearth, the buried heart of the original Flame, deep beneath the dead capital.

The final descent. The Hearth is not a dungeon of monsters but a dungeon of *memory*: each floor reenacts a moment from the journey, giving each companion one last scene to speak their truth. The Rustkin here are not hostile — they are the *accumulated refusals* of every Vessel who turned back. The final boss is not a person. It is the **Compact itself**, manifest: a vast, rusted furnace-heart that asks, in a voice made of every Vessel who has ever burned, what Aric has come to do.

The player's accumulated choices — kingdoms saved or let go, fragments returned or released, companions' counsel taken or refused — determine which of the endings is *earned*. The boss fight's shape changes accordingly.

**Arc conclusion (grand conclusion).** Three endings, each a valid answer to the main theme:

1. **The Rekindling.** Aric burns. The Flame returns, the kingdoms begin to heal, and the party carries his memory into a world that will one day forget him. A warm ending with a cold bill.
2. **The Release.** Aric refuses the compact and shatters the Hearth. The Flame ends. The world grows colder, harder, and *free* — humanity must now warm itself. A cold ending with a warm bill.
3. **The Third Fire.** Only unlocked by a journey that refused every easy answer. Aric and his companions **share** the compact among themselves — no single Vessel, no extinguished world, but a distributed, mortal, fragile flame carried by many. The age of kingdoms ends; the age of people begins. The rust does not vanish; it is simply no longer the whole story.

Whichever ending is reached, the camera rests last on the shrine in Ardel — rebuilt, or not rebuilt, or rebuilt differently — and on a child warming their hands at whatever fire remains.

## Thematic Throughlines (for content authors)

- **Rust is not evil, it is *tired*.** Enemies, NPCs, and ruins should feel exhausted rather than malicious. Even the Cinder Marshal is tired, not cruel.
- **Every kingdom is a thesis.** Ardel = innocence, Millhaven = denial, Harborgate = opportunism, Ruinwatch = resignation, Frostholm = preservation-at-any-cost, Ashenveil = acceptance. The party's arc is the rebuttal the world has been waiting for.
- **Companions are not sidekicks, they are *arguments*.** Elise argues for knowing, Jep for enduring, Reiya for witnessing, Kael for keeping faith. Their late-game dialogue should reflect which argument the player has been listening to.
- **The flame is the player.** Every scene should ultimately be about *what the player chooses to carry forward*, not about what the world demands of them.
